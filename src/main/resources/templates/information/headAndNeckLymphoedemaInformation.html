What is Head and Neck Lymphoedema?

Head and neck lymphoedema is an accumulation of fluid in the tissues of the head and neck region, often resulting from surgery or radiation therapy associated with cancer treatment. This condition leads to swelling and can impact daily activities. Early recognition and management are critical to improving outcomes and maintaining quality of life.

Causes

Head and neck lymphoedema is typically caused by damage to the lymphatic system due to surgery or radiation therapy for cancer. This damage impairs the system’s ability to drain lymphatic fluid, leading to swelling. Other causes can include infections, injuries, or congenital abnormalities that affect lymphatic drainage.

Symptoms

  • Persistent swelling in the face, neck, or around the eyes
  • A feeling of fullness or tightness in the affected areas
  • Changes in skin texture
  • Reduced flexibility in the neck or jaw
  • Difficulty with daily activities due to swelling or discomfort
  • Symptoms that worsen in the morning and improve throughout the day

Risk Factors

The risk of developing head and neck lymphoedema increases with the following factors:

  • Extensive surgery or radiation therapy in the head and neck region
  • Obesity or being overweight
  • Infections or injuries to the lymphatic system
  • Pre-existing venous conditions
  • Lack of physical activity following surgery

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is made through a combination of physical examination and imaging techniques, such as lymphoscintigraphy, to visualize the lymphatic system. Measurements and patient history also play crucial roles in assessing the extent of lymphoedema and planning appropriate treatment.

Guides

Taking Measurements

Accurate measurements are essential for tracking the progression and management of lymphoedema. Use a flexible tape measure to gauge the circumference of the affected areas consistently over time. Document these measurements regularly and share them with your healthcare provider.

Taking Photos

Photographs can help track changes in swelling and symptoms. Use consistent lighting and angles, and take photos at regular intervals, such as weekly or monthly, to monitor the condition effectively.

Performing Exercises

Neck Rotations

Lie on your back with one pillow supporting your head. Slowly turn your head to one side until you feel a gentle stretch on the opposite side. Hold for 20 seconds. Repeat on the other side.

To progress, use the opposite hand on the side of the face to provide a gentle stretch.

Neck Side Flexion

Sit or stand to do this exercise. Tilt your head to one side so that you are bringing your ear nearer to the tip of your shoulder. Hold for 20 seconds. Repeat on the opposite side.

To progress, use the opposite hand to gently apply pressure to your head and add a stretch.

Neck Extension/Flexion

Sit upright. Slowly take your head backwards and hold for 2 seconds. Support your head with your hand if you find this more comfortable. Then, slowly take your head forward to bring your chin towards your chest. Hold for 2 seconds.

To progress, place your hands at the back of your head and gently add pressure when tilting your head down.

Shoulder Shrug and Rolls

Sit upright with your arms relaxed. Shrug your shoulders up towards your ears, hold for a count of 2, then slowly return to the starting position.

Arm Lifts

Lie on your back with one pillow supporting your head and put your arms by your sides. Holding your hands, lift your arms above your head keeping the elbows straight. Hold for up to 20 seconds.

To increase stretch, remove the pillow or hold a small weight like a soup can in your hands.

Shoulder Rotation

Lie on your back with one pillow supporting your head. Clasp your hands behind your neck and point your elbows to the ceiling. Let your elbows fall out to the sides so that you feel a stretch in the front of your chest. Hold for 20 seconds.

Shoulder Blade Squeezes

Sit upright with your arms out to the side. Squeeze your shoulder blades together and hold for a count of five, then relax.

Donning and Doffing Compression Garments

Compression garments must be applied and removed carefully to avoid discomfort and skin damage. Use gentle, steady motions and donning aids if necessary. Consult with your healthcare provider for proper use and fit.

Using Compression Garments

Compression garments help manage swelling by applying consistent pressure to the affected area. They should be worn as directed by your healthcare provider and checked regularly for fit and effectiveness.

Treatment

Treatment for head and neck lymphoedema typically involves a combination of physical therapy, compression garments, and exercises. Manual lymphatic drainage (MLD) is a specialized massage technique that may also be recommended. Consistent management is key to improving outcomes and maintaining quality of life.

Skin and Oral Care

Proper skin care is crucial to prevent infections such as cellulitis:

  • Moisturize the neck, chin, and cheeks daily with a non-perfumed moisturizer. Apply with gentle downward strokes towards your neck and collar bone.
  • Keep your skin hydrated by drinking plenty of fluids.
  • Use non-perfumed products to cleanse the skin. Pat the skin dry; do not rub or cause friction.
  • Use a high-factor sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher) when exposed to sunlight.
  • Avoid shaving with a razor; use an electric shaver to reduce the risk of cuts. Avoid facial waxing, electrolysis, or facial bleach.
  • Treat any skin breaks promptly with antiseptic cream.

Manual and Simple Lymphatic Drainage

Manual lymphatic drainage is a gentle massage technique that helps move excess fluid from swollen areas to where the lymphatic system is functioning well. This should be done daily and may be a long-term requirement.

Exercises for Head and Neck Lymphoedema

It is important to keep the head, neck, and shoulders mobile where possible throughout and after treatment. Specific exercises can be given to encourage movement that can help improve swallowing and lymphatic drainage.

  • Deep breaths – take 5 deep breaths in through the nose and out through the mouth.
  • Shrug shoulders 10 times.
  • Push shoulders forward and back - repeat 10 times.
  • Push chin forwards and then back to create a double chin - repeat 10 times.
  • Turn head to the left - repeat 10 times.
  • Turn head to the right - repeat 10 times.
  • Take your left ear to your left shoulder - repeat 10 times.
  • Now do the same with your right ear - repeat 10 times.
  • Look up at the ceiling - repeat 10 times.
  • Now look down to the floor - repeat 10 times (make sure chin is tucked in).
  • Now say your vowels – AEIOU in an exaggerated manner - repeat 5 times.
  • Smile 5 times.
  • Poke out your tongue as far as you can - repeat 5 times.
  • Open your mouth as wide as you can - repeat 5 times.
  • Frown and screw up your eyes and face - repeat 5 times.

Garments

Compression garments are a common treatment for lymphoedema. For head and neck oedema, options include collars or bands to apply gentle pressure and assist lymphatic drainage. Your therapist will look at the best options for you and advise when to wear this.

Kinesio Tape

Kinesio tape can help lymphatic drainage by lifting the skin in the affected area or as a support/correction strip. It can also be used for scar/fibrosed tissue.

Positioning

Lymphoedema in the head and neck often worsens overnight. Raise the head of your bed or use pillows to elevate your head. A wedge pillow may also help with positioning.
